# Class plan

Based on 'What the Best College Teachers Do by Ken Bain'

Bains practical teaching applications for each core insight.

---

## 🎯 1. Learning = Making Meaning → Work with Conceptual Questions

**What does this mean didactically?**
Stop “covering content” and start provoking thinking.

### Concrete applications:

* Start a lesson with a **big, stimulating question**

  > “Why do some economies fall into recession precisely when governments try to stimulate them?”

* Use *why* and *how* questions instead of *what* questions.

* Let students formulate a hypothesis before you explain the theory.

* Use cases where knowledge must be applied in new contexts.

👉 Goal: students must adjust their mental models, not just store information.

---

## 🧠 2. Make Misconceptions Explicit → Diagnose Before Instruction

**What does this mean didactically?**
You need to know what students already think.

### Concrete applications

* Start with a short **prior knowledge check** (e.g., Mentimeter, statements, mini-quiz).
* Ask students to make a prediction before a demonstration or explanation.
* Use “thinking error” questions:

  > “Why do many people think X works, when in fact it doesn’t?”

👉 Make misconceptions visible and discuss them explicitly.

---

## 🌊 3. Promote Deep Learning → Design for Transfer

**What does this mean didactically?**
Assessment determines how students learn.

### Concrete applications

* Ask questions that require students to:

  * make connections
  * apply theory to new situations
  * weigh arguments

* Use open-ended questions instead of only multiple choice.

* Ask students to explain *why* their answer is correct.

👉 For every assignment, ask yourself:
“Does this require reproduction or thinking?”

---

## 🔥 4. Intrinsic Motivation → Create a “Natural Critical Learning Environment”

Bain considers this crucial.

### In practice

* Start from an **authentic problem** instead of from theory.

* Formulate learning objectives as questions:

  * Not: “We will cover Chapter 3”
  * But: “How can you determine whether a source is reliable?”

* Provide autonomy:

  * Choice of topic
  * Choice of working method
  * Choice of presentation format

👉 Students should feel: *this is intellectually meaningful*.

---

## ❌ 5. Make Mistakes Productive → Normalize Uncertainty

### Concrete applications

* Respond to mistakes with:

  > “Interesting — can you explain how you arrived at that?”

* Let students first discuss in pairs before answering in plenary.

* Use ungraded “concept checks.”

* Discuss sample answers that include errors.

👉 Safety + challenge = growth.

---

## 🪞 6. Integrate Metacognition → Get Students to Think About Their Thinking

### Concrete applications

* End lessons with questions such as:

  * “What was the most difficult concept today?”
  * “What do you understand differently now compared to the beginning?”

* Ask students to write a short reflection with an assignment:

  * “What went well?”
  * “What would you do differently next time?”

* Ask: “How do you know that you understand this?”

👉 This strengthens transfer and long-term learning.

---

## 📈 7. High Expectations + Trust

### Concrete applications

* Communicate explicitly:

  > “This is difficult. That’s normal. And I expect you to be able to do this.”

* Give feedback focused on strategy, not talent:

  * Not: “You’re smart.”
  * But: “Your analysis became stronger because you incorporated multiple perspectives.”

👉 Growth mindset in action.

---

## 🧩 Putting It All Together: What Does Such a Lesson Look Like?

A Bain-inspired lesson:

1. Start with a challenging problem.
2. Students formulate initial ideas.
3. Misconceptions become visible.
4. Theory is introduced as a thinking tool.
5. Application to a new situation.
6. Reflection on how their thinking has changed.
