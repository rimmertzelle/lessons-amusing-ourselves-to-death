# Answer Key & Grading Notes
## Technology That Makes Us Dumber — Or Does It?
*Teacher-facing document. Not for distribution to students.*

## Exercise 1 — The Feed Experiment

**Model answer elements:**
- Students should find that the majority of content is emotionally charged rather than factually grounded or argumentatively structured
- The "who decided" question should lead to the concept of algorithmic curation based on prior engagement — not editorial judgment or relevance
- Strong pairs will connect this to Postman's "peek-a-boo world": information appears, triggers a reaction, and disappears without consequence or follow-up

**Grading notes:**
- *Weak:* Student describes what they saw without connecting it to the question of control or design intent
- *Adequate:* Student identifies algorithmic curation and notes it affects what they see, but treats it as neutral
- *Strong:* Student recognizes that the algorithm is not neutral — it is optimized for a specific outcome (engagement) that may conflict with informational value; begins to question who benefits from this design

## Exercise 2 — Design Against Postman

**Model answer elements (any option):**

*Option A (autoplay):*
- Increases engagement by removing friction; users watch more because stopping requires active effort
- Trains users to expect content to come to them passively; reduces deliberate choice
- Postman would say this deepens the "peek-a-boo" dynamic — content flows without the user ever committing attention to any single piece

*Option B (push notifications):*
- Exploits urgency bias; "trending" triggers FOMO
- Trains users to treat recency as a proxy for importance
- Postman would note this mirrors the TV news cycle: everything is "breaking," nothing has context

*Option C (similar-user recommendations):*
- Reinforces existing preferences; effective at maximizing short-term satisfaction
- Trains users to expect confirmation rather than challenge; narrows the information diet
- Postman would connect this to the displacement of rational discourse by emotional resonance

**Grading notes:**
- *Weak:* Student describes the technical mechanism but does not connect it to user behavior or Postman's framework
- *Adequate:* Student identifies how the feature shapes user behavior and makes a surface-level Postman connection
- *Strong:* Student articulates the tension between business logic (engagement metrics) and informational integrity, and takes a defensible position on whether this is a problem worth solving

## Exercise 3 — Huxley or Orwell?

**Model answers:**

| Scenario | Label | Key reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| A (facial recognition) | Orwellian | Coercive surveillance by state power; fear and force as control mechanism |
| B (streaming algorithm, 4 hrs/day) | Huxleyan | No coercion; users choose to stay; pleasure and habit as control |
| C (silent post removal) | Orwellian with Huxleyan elements | Censorship (Orwell), but the invisibility of it (no notification) has a Huxleyan quality — users don't even know they're being controlled |
| D (outrage headlines, business decision) | Huxleyan | No political intent; purely commercial; users engage willingly; control through preference |

**Key discussion point:** Scenario C and D are deliberately ambiguous. Strong students will note that the corporation/government distinction matters less than the *mechanism* of control — and that Postman's warning is specifically about the cases where no one feels controlled.

**Grading notes:**
- *Weak:* Student applies labels without explanation, or conflates the two concepts
- *Adequate:* Student correctly labels most scenarios with reasonable justification
- *Strong:* Student engages with the ambiguous cases (C, D), questions whether the government/corporation distinction is meaningful, and connects to Postman's core argument about the invisibility of Huxleyan control

## Exercise 4 — Audit Your Own Media Diet

**Key elements a strong answer includes:**
- Honest engagement with the question (not a defensive or dismissive response)
- Recognition that most recalled content tends to be emotionally salient rather than argumentatively rich
- Some reflection on the difference between *sought* and *surfaced* content
- Connection to at least one concept from the lesson (peek-a-boo, entertainment mind, attention economy)

**Grading notes:**
- *Weak:* Student lists media consumed without reflecting on what it means or connecting to course concepts
- *Adequate:* Student identifies patterns in their consumption and makes a connection to Postman, but the reflection is general
- *Strong:* Student observes something specific and unexpected about their own media diet, connects it precisely to a concept from the lesson, and shows genuine intellectual discomfort or curiosity

## Exercise 5 — Letter to a Future Developer

**Key elements a strong answer includes:**
- Accurate, concise summary of Postman's core warning (not just "TV is bad" — the deeper point about how media shapes cognition and public discourse)
- A specific, plausible professional example (not vague: "we should be careful with technology")
- A principle or question that is actionable and reflects genuine ethical reasoning — e.g., "Before we ship, ask: does this feature serve the user's long-term attention, or exploit their short-term engagement?"
- Own voice and position — the student is not required to agree with Postman but must engage seriously

**Grading notes:**
- *Weak:* Student summarizes Postman vaguely ("he said media is bad") and gives a generic developer tip
- *Adequate:* Student summarizes the argument accurately and connects it to a plausible professional scenario
- *Strong:* Student synthesizes Postman's framework into a principle that is specific, actionable, and reflects the student's own professional identity as an IT developer; shows awareness of the tension between commercial incentives and informational integrity

## Exercise 6 — Counter-argument

**Model answer structure:**

*What is strong about the counter-argument:*
- The internet does provide access to more primary sources, academic papers, long-form journalism, and diverse viewpoints than any previous medium
- Media literacy is a real and important concept — users are not entirely passive
- Postman's framework was developed for broadcast television, a one-to-many medium; the internet allows many-to-many communication, which is structurally different

*What Postman would say in response:*
- Access to serious information is not the same as serious engagement with it
- The question is not what is *available* but what the medium's dominant logic *rewards* — and on social platforms, that logic rewards speed, emotion, and brevity over sustained argument
- Media literacy assumes a stable, rational subject who can evaluate sources; Postman's point is that the medium shapes the subject itself — it changes what people expect and want from information

*Defensible student positions include:*
- Postman is largely right: the problem is structural, not literacy-based
- The counter-argument is partially right: the internet is not monolithic; long-form media thrives alongside short-form
- Both: the medium shapes attention, but users are not helpless — media literacy matters *and* design matters

**Grading notes:**
- *Weak:* Student takes a side without engaging the other; does not use Postman's actual argument
- *Adequate:* Student identifies valid points on both sides and states a position
- *Strong:* Student recognizes that Postman's argument operates at the level of *cognitive habit and expectation*, not individual access — and that this makes the media literacy counter-argument more complicated than it first appears
