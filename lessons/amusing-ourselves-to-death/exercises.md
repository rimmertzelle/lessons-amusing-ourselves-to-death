# Exercises: Technology That Makes Us Dumber — Or Does It?

---

## During the Lesson (In-class, in pairs)

---

### Exercise 1 — The Feed Experiment (10 min)
*Targets: surface misconceptions, prior knowledge activation*

Open any social media platform you use regularly (Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, X, Reddit — your choice). Scroll for exactly **60 seconds** without interacting.

Then answer with your partner:

1. How many posts did you see that contained a verifiable fact or argument you could follow up on?
2. How many were designed to trigger an emotional reaction (anger, awe, humor, fear)?
3. Who decided what you saw — and based on what?

Discuss: **Is this a tool you control, or a tool that controls you?**

> *Targets: misconception about technology neutrality; connects to Postman's "peek-a-boo world"*
> *Teacher note: If students don't have phones or prefer not to use them, use a screenshot set prepared in advance.*

---

### Exercise 2 — Design Against Postman (10 min)
*Targets: application of theory to professional context*

You and your partner are junior developers at a tech company. Your team lead says:

> "We need to increase daily active users by 20% next quarter. Here are three ideas on the table — pick one and build it."

**Option A:** Add an autoplay feature that starts the next video 3 seconds after the current one ends.
**Option B:** Send push notifications when a topic the user has engaged with is "trending."
**Option C:** Redesign the home screen to show content based on what similar users found most engaging.

Pick one option. Then answer:
1. How does this feature increase engagement?
2. What does it *train* users to expect from information?
3. Would Postman say this is a problem? Would you?

> *Targets: deep learning / transfer — applying Postman's framework to real design decisions*
> *Teacher note: There is no "correct" option to pick. The exercise is about the analysis, not the choice.*

---

### Exercise 3 — Huxley or Orwell? (10 min)
*Targets: conceptual connection, argumentation*

Postman argues Huxley was right: we are not oppressed by what we fear, but distracted by what we love.

Read the following two scenarios with your partner and decide: is each one more **Orwellian** (control through force/fear) or **Huxleyan** (control through pleasure/distraction)?

**Scenario A:** A government uses facial recognition cameras to monitor citizens in public spaces and flags dissidents automatically.

**Scenario B:** A streaming platform's algorithm keeps users watching for an average of 4 hours per day by serving increasingly emotionally intense content — no coercion involved.

**Scenario C:** A social platform removes posts that contain certain political keywords without notifying the poster.

**Scenario D:** A news app learns that users engage 3× longer with outrage-inducing headlines and starts prioritizing them — purely as a business decision.

For each: label it, explain your reasoning in one sentence, and discuss whether it matters *who* is in control — a government or a corporation.

> *Targets: conceptual transfer, ethical reasoning*

## After the Lesson (Homework, individual)

---

### Exercise 4 — Audit Your Own Media Diet (10 min)
*Targets: metacognition, transfer to personal context*

Think back over the last 48 hours. Without looking at your phone — from memory:

1. Name **three things** you learned from media (social media, news, video, podcasts) that you still remember right now.
2. For each: how did you encounter it? Was it part of a sustained argument or a standalone fragment?
3. Were any of these things you actively sought out, or did an algorithm surface them?

Write a short reflection (150–200 words):
**"What does my own media diet say about the kind of information I am being trained to expect?"**

> *Targets: metacognition, Postman's typographic vs. entertainment mind*
> *Teacher note: Emphasize there are no right answers — this is about honest self-observation.*

---

### Exercise 5 — Letter to a Future Developer (10 min)
*Targets: deep learning, transfer, ethical reasoning*

Write a short letter (200–250 words) from yourself — as a future IT professional — to a junior developer joining your team five years from now.

In the letter, explain:
- What Postman's core warning is, in your own words
- One concrete way it applies to the technology your (imaginary) company builds
- One principle or question you think every developer should ask before shipping a feature

You may agree or disagree with Postman — but you must engage seriously with his argument.

> *Targets: deep learning / transfer — requires synthesis, own voice, professional context*

---

### Exercise 6 — Counter-argument (10 min)
*Targets: critical thinking, argumentation, productive disagreement*

Postman wrote *Amusing Ourselves to Death* in 1985, about television. Some critics argue his framework does not apply — or applies very differently — to the internet and social media.

Write a short response (150–200 words) to the following claim:

> "Postman was right about TV, but wrong about the internet. The internet actually gives people more access to serious information than ever before. The problem isn't the medium — it's media literacy."

Your response should:
1. Identify what is **strong** about this counter-argument
2. Identify what Postman would say in response
3. State your own position clearly

> *Targets: argumentation, nuanced thinking, resisting binary conclusions*
