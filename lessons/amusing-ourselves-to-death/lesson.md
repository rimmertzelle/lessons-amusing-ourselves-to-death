# Lesson: Technology That Makes Us Dumber — Or Does It?

**Target audience:** First-year IT bachelor students (no prior media theory background assumed)
**Duration:** 90 minutes
**Learning objectives (as questions):**
- How do the technologies we build and use shape the way we think — not just what we think about?
- Why does making information entertaining often make it less useful?
- As future IT professionals, what responsibility do we carry for the systems we design?

---

## 1. Opening — Challenging Problem or Question

**Big opening question (show on screen, no preamble):**

> "You are scrolling through your feed for 40 minutes without learning anything you can use tomorrow. Is that your fault — or the app's fault?"

Give students 2 minutes to silently consider, then ask for a show of hands:
- "Whose fault is it? Yours? The app's? Both? Neither?"

Do **not** resolve the debate. Let the tension sit. Tell them: *"By the end of this lesson, you'll have a sharper answer — and it might surprise you."*

**Bridge to Postman:**
Neil Postman asked a version of this question in 1985 — before the internet. He was watching TV news. His book *Amusing Ourselves to Death* argues that the medium we use to communicate doesn't just carry messages — it *shapes* what kinds of messages are even possible.

---

## 2. Prior Knowledge Check

**Quick poll or Mentimeter (3 minutes):**

Ask students to rate their agreement (1–5) with the following statements:

1. "Social media platforms are neutral tools — how we use them is up to us."
2. "Algorithms show me what I want to see."
3. "Clickbait and fake news exist because people are lazy or gullible."
4. "If news is entertaining, that's a good thing — more people will watch it."

Capture responses visually. Revisit these at the end of the lesson.

**Purpose:** Surface two core misconceptions:
- That technology is neutral (it is not — design embeds values)
- That the problem is users, not systems (Postman's point is structural)

---

## 3. Core Concepts

### 3.1 — The Medium Is the Message (5 min)

Postman builds on McLuhan: every medium has a built-in *bias*. A telegram favors brevity. A novel favors complexity and patience. Television favors **image, speed, and emotion** — not argument.

> Key concept: **"The medium is the metaphor."**
> What medium you use to think with determines what kinds of thoughts are possible.

**Concrete example:** Try explaining a calculus proof via emoji. Try conveying grief via a pie chart. The form constrains the content.

### 3.2 — The Typographic Mind vs. The Entertainment Mind (10 min)

Postman argues that print culture (books, pamphlets, newspapers) trained people to:
- Follow sustained arguments
- Tolerate ambiguity and delay
- Evaluate evidence over time

Television — and now social media — trains people to:
- React quickly to images and emotions
- Expect resolution in seconds
- Treat everything as content to be consumed and forgotten

**The "peek-a-boo" world:** Information appears, triggers a reaction, disappears — with no context, no follow-up, no consequence.

> Discussion prompt (pairs, 3 min):
> "What does your TikTok / Instagram feed train you to expect from information?"

### 3.3 — When Everything Becomes Entertainment (10 min)

Postman's core thesis: in a media culture dominated by entertainment, **all public discourse gets reshaped to fit the entertainment format** — including:

- News (infotainment: attractive anchors, dramatic music, no analysis)
- Politics (soundbites, personality over policy)
- Education (gamified apps, engagement over understanding)
- Religion (televangelism, megachurch spectacle)

**For IT students — the 2025 version:**

| Postman's 1985 example | 2025 equivalent |
|---|---|
| TV news becoming show business | Algorithmic newsfeeds optimizing for outrage |
| Political ads replacing debate | Deepfake campaign videos |
| Celebrities endorsing politicians | Influencers as political opinion leaders |
| Attention as a commodity | The attention economy; engagement metrics |

**Key concept to introduce:** The **attention economy** — platforms don't sell products to users; they sell *users' attention* to advertisers. Engagement (time-on-app) is the metric. Outrage, fear, and novelty drive engagement better than nuance.

### 3.4 — Huxley, Not Orwell (5 min)

Postman opens his book with a contrast:

- **Orwell's 1984:** We are oppressed by what we *fear* — surveillance, censorship, force
- **Huxley's Brave New World:** We are controlled by what we *love* — pleasure, distraction, comfort

Postman's warning: *"Huxley, not Orwell, was right."*

> We don't need a Big Brother to keep us uninformed.
> We just need an algorithm that knows we'll watch one more video.

## 4. Application

**Small group activity (15 min):**

Divide into groups of 3–4. Each group gets one design scenario:

**Scenario A:** You are designing a news app. Your KPI is "daily active users." What features do you build? What do you sacrifice?

**Scenario B:** You are building an AI content recommendation system for a school. It learns what students engage with most. What could go wrong?

**Scenario C:** You are a developer at a social platform. Your manager asks you to A/B test a feature that increases session time by showing more emotionally provocative content. What do you do?

**Scenario D:** You are tasked with designing a political information app for the next election. How do you present information without it becoming entertainment?

Each group presents their analysis in 2 minutes. Class discusses: *Where does Postman's argument show up in your scenario?*

---

## 5. Reflection

**Return to the opening poll (5 min):**
Revisit the four statements from the Prior Knowledge Check. Ask: *"Has your answer changed? Why?"*

**Metacognitive closing questions (individual, written — 3 min):**

1. "Before today, I thought the problem with misinformation was… Now I think…"
2. "As someone who will build technology: what is one thing you want to keep in mind from today's lesson?"
3. "What question does today's lesson leave you with?"

Collect written reflections (or use a shared doc). These can feed directly into a follow-up discussion or assignment.
